Carbon From Above

Orbital Diamond Fabrication from Asteroid Carbon Feedstock

Speculative Pre-Experimental Framework Paper 2026–2050
Abstract

Orbital AI compute demands radiation-hard substrates beyond silicon's limits. Diamond excels with a 5.5 eV bandgap for SEU resistance, ~2,000 W/mK thermal conductivity, and UV–IR transparency for photonics. This paper proposes a closed-loop orbital chain—ureilite asteroid carbon (2–6 wt%, averaging ~3 wt%) via pyrolysis to solar-powered CVD—structured in three phases (2026–2050) converging ISRU, solar manufacturing, and diamond electronics. A terrestrial validation scaffolding of three experimental tracks precedes orbital deployment, with an autonomous dual-loop spectroscopic control architecture governing real-time CVD quality.

01

Substrate Imperative

Silicon chips launched from Earth inherit terrestrial fab limits, vulnerable to cosmic ray single-event upsets (SEUs) that flip bits despite hardening overhead. Diamond's 5.5 eV bandgap inherently resists such upsets—versus silicon's 1.1 eV. Ureilites offer orbital carbon at 2–6 wt% (averaging ~3 wt%; Downes et al. 2008), microgravity CVD has 1995 precedent, and solar flux supplies energy. Integration unlocks the pathway; the constituent technologies exist.

02

Diamond Superiority

```

2.1 — Radiation Resilience

SEU cross-section drops with bandgap; diamond ignores fluxes that corrupt silicon. Grain boundaries pose residual risks, addressable via defect engineering. Radiation-hard silicon sacrifices 30–50% die area to redundancy—diamond reclaims it at the material level.

2.2 — Thermal Edge

Diamond conducts heat at ~2,000 W/mK versus silicon's ~150 W/mK—a 13× advantage critical in vacuum without convective cooling. GaN-on-diamond prototypes demonstrate high-power viability at the substrate, not the package.

2.3 — Photonic Fit

Full-spectrum UV–IR transparency enables hybrid electro-photonic compute, directly suited to volumetric AI accelerator architectures.

```

Orbital Feedstock

```

3.1 — Ureilite Composition

Ureilite asteroids from disrupted differentiated bodies contain 2–6 wt% carbon averaging ~3 wt% (Grady et al. 1985; Downes et al. 2008; Walte et al. 2025), distributed as graphite, lonsdaleite, and minor diamond. Graphite is the dominant and most processable CVD feedstock phase. Silicate matrix and metallic contaminants (Fe, S) are chemically separable.

3.2 — Pyrolysis Route

H₂-assisted thermal cracking mirrors established ISRU propellant chemistry. The primary challenge is purification to electronics-grade standard (<1 ppm N, <100 ppb metals)—the physics is not novel. See Section 12, Track A for the terrestrial validation experiment.

04

Solar Furnace Energy

Orbital irradiance of 1,366 W/m² via 500 m² deployable optics meets CVD's kilowatt-scale demand, yielding an estimated ~2 kg diamond per year at zero fuel cost. Epistemic status: physically motivated estimate — not experimentally validated. This inverts terrestrial energy economics. Deployable concentrator technology is maturing through programs including ARAQYS (2025–2027).

7.4 — Autonomous Process Control: Dual-Loop Architecture

In an orbital foundry without crew intervention, the control system must function simultaneously as technician, scientist, and quality officer. The proposed SOLBAKKEN-1 architecture uses two spectroscopic feedback loops operating in parallel, treating plasma not as a static reaction but as a dynamic fluid requiring continuous tuning against variable asteroid feedstock composition.

Loop 1 — Pre-Growth Gatekeeper (OES)
Optical Emission Spectroscopy monitors the plasma's atomic and molecular electronic transitions upstream of the diamond seed. The AI tracks the 388 nm CN emission line as the primary nitrogen contamination indicator. If the CN-to-Hα ratio exceeds 10⁻⁶, an automated purge-and-reclean cycle diverts feedstock gas to a secondary catalytic purification stage before it reaches the growth surface.

Loop 2 — In-Situ Lattice Inspector (Raman / PL)
A 532 nm green laser probes the growing diamond film continuously. Raman spectroscopy tracks the 1332 cm⁻¹ diamond peak; broadening or shift indicates internal stress or graphitic inclusions. Photoluminescence monitoring detects NV-center zero-phonon lines at 575 nm and 637 nm — the most sensitive indicator of nitrogen incorporation into the solid lattice.

The etch-back response to NV formation carries a yield cost — each etch-back cycle removes material already grown. Terrestrial Track B experiments (§12) will quantify cycle frequency and yield loss to determine whether upstream purification targets need tightening to make etch-back operationally viable.

10

Landscape Fit

Terrestrial silicon remains viable short-term but encounters hard limits at orbital scale: radiation flux, launch mass constraints, and the absence of convective cooling. This framework converges three maturing streams—orbital solar (ARAQYS, 2025), ISRU chemistry (NASA), and diamond electronics (GaN-on-diamond, Diamond Quanta 2025)—none of which individually solves the substrate problem. The Terafab announcement (March 2026) targeting a terawatt of orbital compute sharpens the question: silicon's radiation overhead becomes a structural tax at that scale.

Terrestrial Validation Scaffolding

Three experimental tracks gate the program before any orbital activity. Tracks A and B are Phase 1 activities executable with existing laboratory infrastructure. Track C is deferred to Phase 2, gated on Track A and B success.

```
Track A Feedstock Chemistry Phase 1 · Terrestrial
Can ureilite simulant pyrolysis produce a purifiable CH₄/H₂ stream?
Two-tier simulant strategy. Baseline: published ureilite composition (Mittlefehldt et al. 1998) — ~3 wt% graphitic carbon in olivine/pyroxene matrix with minor troilite. Stress-test: worst-case composition at 6 wt% carbon with elevated sulfur content, within documented ureilite range. Hydrogen-assisted thermal pyrolysis at 900–1200°C across multiple temperature steps. Gas output analyzed by mass spectrometry and FTIR at each step.
Pass criteria: N < 1 ppm · S < 100 ppb · Metal carbonyls below detection · CH₄/H₂ ratio within CVD-compatible range
Kill condition: Stress-test simulant cannot be purified to threshold through single-stage catalytic filter.
Track B Control Loop Phase 1 · Terrestrial
Can the AI steer diamond growth from contaminated feedstock using spectroscopy alone?
Commercial CVD chamber instrumented with OES fiber (CN 388 nm, Hα 656 nm) and 532 nm Raman/PL probe. Rule-based decision engine implements the §7.4 matrix as the Phase 1 control architecture. Three growth trials: (1) clean feedstock as baseline, (2) intentional nitrogen spike at known concentration, (3) Track A stress-test gas — real asteroid-analogue contamination, uncharacterized, AI must handle in real time. Every etch-back event logged with associated yield cost — this data informs whether upstream purification targets need tightening.
Pass criteria: CN/Hα below threshold across all trials · Raman FWHM < 2 cm⁻¹ (instrument-calibrated) · Zero detectable NV PL signal
Note: FWHM threshold is a target parameter under validation — requires calibration before operational use.
Track C Microgravity Plasma Phase 2 Opener · Deferred
Does plasma behavior change enough in microgravity to invalidate Tracks A and B?
Deferred to Phase 2 — Track B must succeed terrestrially before microgravity validation is warranted. ISS partnership conversations begin during Phase 1 given 3–5 year access lead time. Preliminary option: drop tower experiment (Bremen ZARM, 4.7 sec; or NASA Glenn, 5.18 sec) to characterize plasma initiation and spatial uniformity without buoyancy-driven convection. Drop tower does not produce meaningful film growth but answers whether plasma geometry collapses without convection — if it does, CVD reactor geometry requires redesign before ISS.
Drop tower question: Does plasma remain stable and spatially uniform when buoyancy convection is removed?
ISS target: Reproduce a Track B steady-state growth run in microgravity; compare film quality metrics.
```
